Abstract
During the Summer of 2020 news media showcased the
negative eects of policing within the United States.
From this, the country has sparked a debate on police ex-
cessive force and militarization within the United States.
This research is on doing a data visualization project that
showcases the excessive force of police militarization
in the United States. For my project, I visualized data
that is centered around the police departments from 3
major cities (New York, Philadelphia, and Washington
D.C). The main aim of this is to compare and contrast
the resource divide within these cities, where I compare
governmental funding and policing funding in the hopes
to tell a story as to why there is a low allocation of re-
sources in those communities. This data visualization
has an abolitionist framework advocating for a defund-
ing of police, reduced militarization, and reallocation
of resources to communities in need. Using data visu-
alization I compare the staunch governmental divide
of funding to the police compared to community-based
funding like mental health resources, aordable housing
infrastructures, and SNAP program recipients. From this
work, a database holds information on grassroots orga-
nizations and anti-racist resources in the hopes to add
accessibility to nding these resources as well as allevi-
ating the need and support of the police. The technology
used HTML, CSS,Node.js, D3.js, Relational Databases,
Heroku, and Github.
Keywords: Human Computer Interaction, Data Visual-
ization, Decolonial Computing

1 Introduction
The United States allocates more money to the police
than they give food to the homeless. With the current
population of the United States resulting in 330 citizens,
the United States policies more than 50 percent of the
population, mostly Black Indigenous People of Color
(BIPOC). From this, I developed a Web application that
captures the lack of resources given to those in need
and tells the story of the need for police abolition. Using
data visualization I compared the staunch governmental
divide of funding to the police compared to community-
based funding like mental health resources, aordable
housing infrastructures,anti-poverty programs, house-
less initiatives and Supplemental Nutrition Assistance
Program (SNAP) program recipients and domestic vio-
lence support. The purpose of choosing these programs
is to showcase the increase in crime based on the de-
crease of community resources. The software develop-
ment methodology was used through an AGILE approach
to better manage the progress of this project. Along
with the software development methodology, the online
website was created using HTML, CSS, D3.js, python,
Heroku and GitHub. Below you will see our system ar-
chitecture.

2.1 Abolitionist Framework
To talk about the framework and lay the foundation of
this capstone project we must discuss the concept of abo-
lition. The idea of abolition is the view of freedom and
true liberation free of the carceral state. Many believe
that abolition is the erasing of the police although this is
the main goal it is important to start small. This can be
promoted in large bodies of knowledge research, defund-
ing the police and community care. In Disbanding the
Police Anthony O’Rourke, Rick Su and Guyora Binder
investigate the reasoning behind such heavy policing
within the United States [
5
] [
4
]. From this work, they
ask the following questions: Why disband police forces,
What are the laws restricting the police? What are the
challenges of law enforcement propaganda and excep-
tionalism? One of the most important pieces of infor-
mation I gathered was the heavy promotion of police
enforcement during the War on Drugs era brought on
by Reaganomics. Although the work in this paper was
especially helpful it fails to visualize the large police
budget within the United States nor did it recognize the
ideas. This, I plan to use this source to gather a broader
understanding of policing in the United States so that I
could communicate my project thoroughly. To continue
with our discussion on abolition Angela Davis has put a
lot of scholarship within the space of abolition. In her
book Are Prisons Obsolete Davis explains the history
of the prison system and explains the idea of a world
without prisons and police is not hard to imagine [
3
].
When she states that, "This is a measure of how dicult
it is to envision a social order that does not rely on the
threat of sequestering people in dreadful places designed
to separate them from their communities and families.
The prison is considered so "natural" that it is extremely
dicult to imagine life without it” (Davis 2) she explains
that slavery, civil rights, and abolitionist ideas towards
prisons; imprisonment, and reform; how gender struc-
tures the prison system; the prison industrial complex
and lastly how we can create an abolitionist future free
of police. When we think of a world free of the carceral
state we must not think punitively. Thinking within a
mindset of punishment is not the Neoliberal alternatives
such as prison reform through the use of house arrest,
better surveillance, or more ’comfortable’ prisons is not
the answer. Through revitalizing our education, and de-
militarizing our schools a free and fair healthcare system
is the way to reduce harm. Davis continues on to say
that the immense support of imprisonment and punitive
punishment from the police “devour public funds, which
might otherwise be available for social programs”[
3
].
This statement is very helpful as it supports my role in
creating a website that showcases the lack of support
for public funding.

4 Final Implementation
This project was dedicated to creating a web application
that told a story of the increased policing within cities of
New York, Philadelphia, and Washington D.C. Through-
out the creation of the web application researchers, com-
munity members and those interested in learning more
about Police abolition are welcome to use the website.
The data gathered was scraped using a python web
scraper and saving it within a CSV le. Referenced is
the data gathered to create the data visualization.
Seen in gure 7 - 9 is the data collected within each
city’s police department. I split the attributes by per-
sonnel service, material services, contractual services
and equipment. Personnel services refer to managing
employee programming, compensation, employee rela-
tions, and police requirements. Material services refer
to the technology used such as facial recognition, bioin-
formatics and surveillance and other tangible forms of
equipment. Contractual services refer to contracts and
human resources-related work. I then grouped this in-
formation and started developing bar charts using d3.js.
The next step after creating the bar chart was to de-
velop the pie chart. The information was gathered using
Open Data.gov for Washington D.C, Philadelphia and
New York City. The attributes compare the police budget
to the social programs allocated to those working in the

5 Results
The completion of this project proved to be successful
as I created the data visualization I wanted to showcase,
gathered the data properly, and listed the additional
resources accurately. Now in this section, I will begin to
explain the results gathered from the data visualization.
5.1 New York City
Seen to the left is the budgeting breaking down for New
York City. New York city’s nearly $99.7 billion takes up
more than 5% of the city’s total budget. Although that 5
% may seem a bit small in correspondence to the social
program budgeted by the city NYPD receive more than
90 % of programs allocated to mental health resources, af-
fordable housing infrastructures,anti-poverty programs,
houseless initiatives, and Supplemental Nutrition Assis-
tance Program (SNAP) program recipients and domestic
violence support. This proves Angela Davis’s point that
police “devour public funds” [3].
5.2 Philadelphia
The data I gathered compares dierently in the data
gathered from the result in New York data visualiza-
tions. Similarly, Philadelphia received immense mon-
etary support y for their police department resulting
in $956,632,151 compared to NYPD’s 5.5 billion. How-
ever, although the NYPD did receive more funding than
Philadelphia due to the high population this didn’t cease
to end the issue. The Philadelphia Police department
still received more funding in community programming
than those seeking help for mental health resources, af-
fordable housing infrastructures,anti-poverty programs,
houseless initiatives, Supplemental Nutrition Assistance
Program (SNAP) program recipients and domestic vio-
lence support.
5.3 Washington D.C
Washington D.C received the least amount of budget-
ing for the Metropolitan Police Department, where they
received less than half of the Philadelphia police depart-
ment and less than a quarter compared to NYPD. How-
ever, regardless of the low budget, they are still granted
more access to resources than the social programs that
need them.

6 Conclusion
To conclude my capstone project I would like to reit-
erate the importance of this work. Through the use of
data visualization, I compared the staunch governmental
divide of funding to the police compared to community-
based funding like mental health resources, aordable
housing infrastructures,anti-poverty programs, house-
less initiatives, Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Pro-
gram (SNAP) program recipients and domestic violence
support. The purpose of choosing these programs is to
showcase the increase in ’crime’ in these cities is based
on the decrease in community resources.
Along with the data visualization, I found grassroots
organizations that follow an abolitionist framework.
This work will add necessary accessibility around
communities’ funding resources using data visualiza-
tion. This project will be used for organizers promoting
abolition and liberation, computational social scientists
for researchers, and community members that would
like to know where their funding is allocated.
The result of this project proves my hypothesis that
the United States dedicates too much money to police
departments and less money to the community. Crime
is subjective and is created by a lack of resources, not
based on the individual. From this information, I argue
along with many abolitionists that if we were to increase
money in the community crime will decrease.
